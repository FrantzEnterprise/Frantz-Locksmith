Safe Burglaries: What Thieves Know That You Don't

How burglars target safes in Sacramento — and how to make sure yours isn't the next one they open.

Published by Frantz Locksmith Service • CA LCO 4160 • Sacramento, CA

I've walked into homes after a burglary and seen the aftermath. A forced door. Ransacked rooms. And in the corner, the safe — either gone entirely or sitting there with the door hanging open, empty. The look on people's faces when they realize their "secure" safe was nothing more than a metal box to a determined thief — I never get used to it.

Let me share what I know about how safes are actually targeted and defeated. This isn't theory. This is what I see in the field.

How Thieves Open Safes

1. Carry It Out

This is the #1 way safes get stolen. A thief walks in, sees a safe under 200 lbs, and walks out with it. They open it later in private with plenty of time, noise, and tools. If your safe isn't bolted down, it's not really secure.

2. Prying

Most consumer-grade safes have a weak point: the gap between the door and the body. A thief inserts a pry bar and leverages the door open. This works in under a minute on most RSC-rated gun safes.

3. Grinding

A cordless angle grinder with a cutting wheel goes through thin safe steel in seconds. Thicker steel takes longer. TL-rated safes have hardened steel plates that resist grinding — but they cost more.

4. Punching the Lock

Some electronic locks can be "punched" — a sharp impact that breaks the internal mechanism and releases the lock. This is why UL-rated locks have relockers as a backup.

Lesson: Floor safes need to be set in the concrete during the pour, not dropped into a cavity afterward. A properly installed floor safe cannot be cut out without hours of work.

What Actually Stops a Thief?

Based on what I see in the field, here's what matters most:

  1. Bolting the safe down. A safe that can't be carried away is a safe that has to be opened on-site. That's harder, louder, and riskier for a thief.
  2. UL burglary rating. RSC-rated safes are a deterrent, not a barrier. TL-15 or higher gives real protection.
  3. Concealment. A safe hidden in a closet under a pile of blankets is harder to find than one sitting in plain sight. But don't rely on concealment alone — if they find it, they'll attack it.
  4. Thick door and body. The door should be at least 1/4" solid steel. The body should be heavy gauge. You can tell a quality safe by how heavy it is for its size.
  5. Relockers. A safe with a proper relocker mechanism survives an attack on the main lock. Most cheap safes don't have one.
⚠️ The hard truth: No safe is 100% burglar-proof. A thief with unlimited time, tools, and privacy will eventually get into anything. The goal is to make your safe take too long and too much effort — so the thief moves on or gets caught trying. A TL-30 rated safe that's bolted down and hidden will make 99% of burglars walk away.

What About Alarms and Cameras?

They help. A monitored alarm with a fast response time is a real deterrent. But here's the reality check: most residential alarm response times are 10-20 minutes. A skilled thief can open an RSC safe in 2-5 minutes. They're gone before the police arrive.

The best approach is layered security: alarm + camera + bolted-down TL-rated safe. Each layer adds time. Each layer makes your property less attractive than the neighbor's.
