Safe Opening Methods: Expert vs. Amateur vs. Destructive

Not everyone who opens a safe knows what they're doing. The difference between $200 and $2,000 comes down to who you call — and when.

Published by Frantz Locksmith Service • CA LCO 4160 • Sacramento, CA

I want to tell you a story.

A few years ago, a woman called me about her father's old floor safe. He'd passed away and nobody knew the combination. Before she called me, she called a "locksmith." The guy showed up with an angle grinder and spent two hours cutting the safe out of the floor. He destroyed it. Charged her $800. The safe — a valuable antique Mosler that had been in the family for 60 years — was scrap metal.

When she called me, I told her: if you'd called me first, I'd have manipulated it open in about 20 minutes. No damage. Cost you $200. And the safe would still be in your family, working perfectly.

This happens all the time. And it's why I wrote this article.

⚠️ The expensive lesson: Who you call to open a safe matters more than you think. There are four ways a safe gets opened. The cost difference between them can be 10x — and the damage can be permanent.

The Four Methods

Method 1: Manipulation 🎯

$150-$350 Non-destructive Requires skill

What it is: The technician turns the dial and listens to the internal wheels as they drop into place. Using sound and feel, they find each number in the combination without ever knowing it beforehand.

Who can do it: Only experienced safe technicians. This is a lost art. Most locksmiths can't do it. Most "safe techs" can't either.

Time: 15 minutes to 2 hours depending on lock type and condition.

Damage: Zero. The safe works perfectly when you're done.

Best for: Antique safes, valuable safes, anything you want to preserve.

Method 2: Precision Drilling 🔧

$250-$500 Minimal damage Requires knowledge

What it is: The technician drills a small hole in a precise location to bypass the lock or reach the wheel pack. The hole is then filled, threaded, and a screw is installed to cover it.

Who can do it: A trained safe technician who knows exactly where to drill for each lock brand and model. One wrong millimeter and you've hit the relocker.

Time: 30-60 minutes.

Damage: Minimal. A single small hole that's professionally patched. Usually invisible.

Best for: When manipulation fails or isn't possible. Most common method for standard safes.

Method 3: Amateur Destructive 🔨

$500-$1,500+ Damages safe Risks contents

What it is: Someone with a pry bar, angle grinder, hammer, and no real plan. They work by brute force. This is what most "locksmiths" do when they don't know safe mechanisms.

Who does it: General locksmiths who don't specialize in safes. Handymen. "I saw it on YouTube" types.

Time: 1-4 hours.

Damage: Significant. The safe body is cut, the door is pried, the lock is destroyed. Often the contents are damaged by grinding sparks or bending of the door.

The real cost: You still need a new safe. Total cost = destruction fee + new safe + new installation.

Method 4: Professional Destructive (Fire/Rescue) 🔥

$200-$500 Last resort Controlled

What it is: Using torches, specialized cutting tools, or even thermal lances to open a safe when non-destructive methods have failed and the contents must be accessed immediately.

Who does it: Professionals who know the safe's construction and cut in specific places to minimize damage and protect contents.

When it's used: Emergency situations (child locked in a safe, evidence access for law enforcement), or safes that are damaged beyond repair anyway.

The key difference from amateur: A pro knows where to cut. An amateur cuts everywhere.

How to Know Who to Call

Not everyone who calls themselves a locksmith knows safes. A general locksmith can rekey your house and install deadbolts — that doesn't mean they know how to manipulate a combination lock.

Here's what to ask before you hire someone to open a safe:

  1. "Do you specialize in safes?" — If they hesitate, call someone else.
  2. "Will you try manipulation first?" — A safe specialist will. A general locksmith will reach for the drill.
  3. "What brand of locks do you work with?" — They should rattle off S&G, LaGard, Kaba Mas without thinking. If they can't name the big three, they're not a safe tech.
  4. "How many safes do you open per month?" — A real safe tech opens 10+. A generalist opens maybe 1-2 a year.
  5. "What happens if you trigger the relocker?" — They should tell you exactly how they'd handle it. If they don't know what a relocker is, hang up.
⚠️ Red flag: If someone says "I can get it open" without asking what kind of safe and lock it is, they're planning to destroy it. A real safe tech needs to know what they're dealing with before they quote you a price.

The Bottom Line

If you're locked out of a safe, there's only one question that matters: do you want to keep this safe, or are you ready to replace it?

If you want to keep it — call a safe specialist. Ask for manipulation first. Pay for the knowledge, not the destruction.

If you don't care about the safe and just need the contents — you can still call a specialist. The precision drilling method is faster and cheaper than the amateur approach. And they won't accidentally destroy what's inside.
