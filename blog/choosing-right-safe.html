Choosing the Right Safe

A practical guide to picking the right safe for your home or business — without getting ripped off or buying more than you need.

Published by Frantz Locksmith Service • CA LCO 4160 • Sacramento, CA

I've been inside a lot of safes over the last 40+ years. Some were beautiful antiques that had been in families for generations. Others were cheap gun safes that I could have pried open with a crowbar and ten minutes of quiet time. The difference between a safe that protects your valuables and a metal box that gives you false confidence comes down to one thing: picking the right one in the first place.

Here's what I've learned about choosing a safe — the practical way, not the salesman way.

Step 1: What Are You Protecting?

This sounds obvious, but most people skip it. They walk into a store, see a safe, and buy it because it looks sturdy. Here's the truth: no single safe does everything well.

  • Paper documents (birth certificates, deeds, wills) — need fire protection first, burglary second
  • Firearms — need burglary protection and interior space, fire rating is a bonus
  • Cash and jewelry — need burglary protection first, fire second
  • Digital media (hard drives, USBs, photos) — need a lower internal temperature rating (UL 150, not 350)
  • Collectibles and heirlooms — need both fire and burglary, plus climate control
⚠️ The biggest mistake I see: Someone buys a $200 "fire safe" from a big box store, bolts it to the floor, and thinks their valuables are secure. That safe can be carried out the door in under 30 seconds with a pry bar. The fire rating might protect the documents, but the safe itself won't be there after a burglary.

Step 2: Understand the Ratings

Safe ratings aren't marketing. They're UL (Underwriters Laboratories) standards — independently tested and certified. If a safe doesn't have a UL sticker, you're trusting the manufacturer's word, and that's worth exactly what you paid for it.

Fire Ratings

RatingWhat It MeansGood For
UL Class 350 1-HourWithstands 1700°F for 1 hour, interior stays under 350°FPaper documents (paper chars at ~400°F)
UL Class 350 2-HourSame, but 2 hours at higher tempsBetter protection, documents in a serious fire
UL Class 150 1-HourInterior stays under 150°FMedia — hard drives, USB drives, photos, film

Most cheap "fire safes" at hardware stores are NOT UL rated. They're tested by the manufacturer or an unaccredited lab. A UL rating costs money to get — if a safe doesn't have one, there's a reason.

💡 Here's what most people don't know: The vast majority of "gun safes" sold today are RSC-rated. That's the lowest UL burglary rating — 5 minutes of basic tool attack. A motivated thief with a crowbar and a sawzall can open most gun safes in under 10 minutes. If you own firearms, buy at minimum a TL-15 rated safe.

Step 3: Size and Placement Matter More Than You Think

A safe that's too small will be frustrating every time you use it. A safe that's too big might not fit where you need it. And a safe that's not bolted down might as well be a delivery box.

Size Guidelines

  • Documents only: 1-2 cubic feet (fits under a desk or in a closet)
  • Handguns + documents: 2-4 cubic feet
  • Long guns (4-6 rifles): 12-18 cubic feet
  • Long guns (8+ rifles): 24+ cubic feet
  • Business cash deposits: Drop safe, 1-3 cubic feet

Where to Put It

  • Floor safes: Best burglary protection, worst for fire (concrete conducts heat). Prone to moisture.
  • Wall safes: Convenient, low security. Easy to cut out of the wall.
  • Closet or garage: Most common. Make sure the floor can support the weight (a loaded gun safe can weigh 800+ lbs).
  • Bolt it down: I can't stress this enough. An unbolted safe can be tipped over, rolled away, or carried out by two people with a dolly.

Step 4: Lock Type — Dial vs. Keypad vs. Biometric

  • Mechanical dial (S&G, LaGard): Most reliable. No batteries. Lasts decades. Requires knowing the sequence (ask my chatbot about dialing sequences).
  • Electronic keypad: Convenient. Faster to open. Batteries die eventually. Most failures are battery-related.
  • Biometric (fingerprint): Least reliable. Great when it works, frustrating when it doesn't. Batteries drain faster. Keep your backup key handy.
My recommendation: For a safe you access daily (business, gun safe), get an electronic keypad. For a safe you access rarely (documents, heirlooms), get a mechanical dial. Skip biometric for now — the technology isn't there yet for long-term reliability.

The Bottom Line

Here's how I'd pick if I were buying a safe today:

For documents: UL 350 2-hour fire safe, mechanical dial, bolted down. AMSEC or Gardall.

For guns: Minimum TL-15 burglary rating, electronic keypad for quick access, at least 12 cubic feet, bolted to concrete. Liberty or AMSEC.

For cash/jewelry at home: TL-15 or TL-30, keypad, moderate fire rating, hidden in a closet and bolted down.

For business deposits: Drop safe with dual locks (one for drop door, one for main door). TL-15 minimum.
