Safes and Child Safety

Safe storage for firearms, keeping children out of locked spaces, and choosing the right lock for a family home.

Published by Frantz Locksmith Service • CA LCO 4160 • Sacramento, CA

I don't get many calls about child safety and safes. But when I do, they're serious. A child who gets locked inside a safe — or finds a gun that wasn't secured — is a call nobody wants to take. I've had both. Once is enough to make this the first thing I mention to any parent buying a safe.

The Risks

Children Locked Inside

It sounds unlikely, but it happens more often than you'd think. Kids play hide-and-seek. An empty or partially empty gun safe looks like a great hiding spot. The door swings shut. The lock engages. And now you have a child trapped in a sealed metal box with limited air.

Most safe doors have an interior release mechanism — a knob, lever, or button that opens the door from the inside. But not all of them do. And the ones that exist are often small, hard to find in the dark, and require strength a small child doesn't have.

⚠️ If you have children: Before you buy a safe, check that it has an interior release mechanism that a child can operate. Test it yourself. Can you find it in the dark? Can a 6-year-old reach it and turn it? If not, find a different safe.

Firearm Access

This is the more talked-about risk — and for good reason. According to the CDC, there are thousands of unintentional shootings by children each year in the United States. Many of them involve guns that the child thought were "hidden" but found anyway, or guns stored in a safe with a combination the child had watched the parent enter.

A safe is only as secure as the person using it. If you enter your combination in front of your kids, they will remember it. Kids are observant. The electronic keypad beeps and lights up — it's fascinating to a child.

Safe Storage Guidelines

For Firearms

  • Use a gun safe designed for quick access — but child-resistant. A biometric or electronic lock with a backup key. Something that opens fast for you but not for small hands.
  • Store ammunition separately. A locked safe for guns and a separate locked container for ammo is the gold standard.
  • Don't rely on hiding. Children find things. The gun under the mattress, on the top shelf, or behind the loose board — kids explore and find everything.
  • Teach safe behavior. A safe can't replace conversations about what to do if a child finds a gun. "Don't touch, tell an adult" — every kid should know this.

For General Safe Ownership with Kids

  • Choose a lock type that's easy for you, hard for them. Electronic locks are the best balance — fast for adults, hard for kids to operate if they don't know the code.
  • Change the code if your child learns it. It happens. Change the code, test it, and move on.
  • Keep the backup key in a true secret spot — not the junk drawer or the key hook in the kitchen.
  • Show older children how the interior release works — this knowledge has saved lives.

Lock Types: Which Is Most Child-Safe?

From the perspective of a parent, here's how the lock types stack up:

  • Electronic keypad: Best. Fast to open, easy to change the code, no physical key for a child to find. Teach your child the code only when they're old enough to be responsible.
  • Biometric: Good, with caveats. Fast access. But a child sleeping — the scanner might not recognize them. Always have a backup key with biometric.
  • Mechanical dial: Decent. No batteries to die. But slower to open. A child who learns the sequence can still open it. At least the dial doesn't beep and light up, so it's less likely to attract attention.
  • Key lock: Least child-safe from a security standpoint. Keys can be found, duplicated, or lost. If you must use a keyed safe, keep the key on your person at all times.

The Bottom Line

If you have children in the house, your safe needs to do two contradictory things: open fast for you in an emergency, but stay completely closed to curious little hands. That's a tough balance.

The right approach is:

  1. Buy a quality safe with an electronic keypad
  2. Make sure it has an interior release a child can operate
  3. Bolt it down so it can't tip over on a child
  4. Store guns and ammo separately
  5. Teach your kids about safe safety
  6. Change the code if they ever learn it

It's not complicated. But it matters. Every day.
