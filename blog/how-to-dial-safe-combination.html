How to Dial a Safe Combination

S&G, LaGard, AMSEC, Group 1, Group 2 — step-by-step instructions from a safe technician with 40+ years experience.

📅 May 2026  •  By Robert Frantz  •  📖 5 min read

If you own a mechanical dial safe and haven't opened it in a while, the instructions taped inside the door are probably faded, missing, or buried under a stack of old tax returns. Dialing a safe is not complicated — but there's a specific rhythm to it, and it varies depending on the lock. Get the pattern wrong and the safe won't open, even if you're entering the right combination.

I've been opening safes since 1985, and I can tell you the most common reason I get called out is not a broken lock — it's someone who forgot the correct dialing sequence. The numbers were right, the technique was wrong.

Here's how to dial the major types of mechanical safe locks, broken down by brand and lock group.

Every Mechanical Lock Has an Opening Index

Before you start dialing, find the opening index mark. This is the fixed reference point on the dial ring — usually a small line, arrow, or triangle (sometimes called a "crow's foot") located at the top, 12 o'clock position. You'll line up each combination number with this index, not the center of the dial.

⚠️ Important: Some older or European safes have the opening index at a different position (2 o'clock or even 4 o'clock). If the numbers don't work at 12 o'clock, look for a second mark. If that fails — call someone who knows.

Group 2 Locks (Standard) — The 4-3-2-1 Pattern

Group 2 is the most common mechanical lock found on residential and commercial safes. Brands like Sargent & Greenleaf LaGard Ilco AMSEC all use this pattern for their three-number combinations:

  1. 4 turns LEFT (counter-clockwise) — pass the first number 3 times and stop on the 4th. This clears the lock and picks up all the wheels.
  2. 3 turns RIGHT (clockwise) — pass the second number 2 times and stop on the 3rd. The wheels start stacking into alignment.
  3. 2 turns LEFT (counter-clockwise) — pass the third number once and stop on the 2nd. The last wheel drops in.
  4. Turn RIGHT slowly until the dial stops (usually between 85 and 95). This retracts the bolt. Pull the handle and open the door.

That's it. Pass the first number 3 times and stop on the 4th, pass the second number 2 times and stop on the 3rd, pass the third number once and stop on the 2nd, then turn right until the dial stops (usually between 85 and 95).

💡 Pro tip from 40+ years: When you turn, go smoothly. Don't jerk the dial or overshoot the number by more than a number or two. If you overshoot, you must start over. Backing up does not work — the wheels will not reset properly.

R-L-R Pattern (Less Common)

A small number of safes are built with the opposite pattern: right-left-right instead of left-right-left. In that case, the same 4-3-2-1 counting applies, but the directions flip:

  1. 4 turns RIGHT — pass first number 3 times, stop on 4th
  2. 3 turns LEFT — pass second number 2 times, stop on 3rd
  3. 2 turns RIGHT — pass third number once, stop on 2nd
  4. Turn LEFT slowly until the dial stops (85–95 range)

If your L-R-L sequence doesn't work, try R-L-R before calling for help.

Group 1 Locks (High Security) — The 5-4-3-2 Pattern

Group 1 locks are higher precision locks used on high-security safes, GSA containers, and bank vaults. They use a 4-number combination and require extra passes because there's an extra wheel in the lock. These are commonly found on Sargent & Greenleaf 8400 and LaGard 3600 series.

  1. 5 turns LEFT — pass first number 4 times, stop on the 5th
  2. 4 turns RIGHT — pass second number 3 times, stop on the 4th
  3. 3 turns LEFT — pass third number 2 times, stop on the 3rd
  4. 2 turns RIGHT — pass fourth number once, stop on the 2nd
  5. Turn LEFT slowly until the dial stops, then open

Alternatively, some Group 1 locks unlock dialing right — the pattern flips just like Group 2 does. If left-right-left-right doesn't work, try right-left-right-left.

🔒 GSA Containers: I'm certified on X-07, X-08, X-09, and X-10 government containers. These use Group 1 locks and the dialing is precise. A half-number off and it won't open. This is where experience matters.

Common Mistakes (And What's Normal)

"I dialed the numbers but nothing happens"

Most common issue: you're not counting passes correctly. You must pass the number on earlier turns — not just land on it. On step 1, you spin past the first number 3 times and land on it the 4th. If you're stopping on the first number without passing it 3 times first, the wheels won't align.

"I overshot the number — can I back up?"

No. Mechanical locks engage a fence-and-lever mechanism that drops into the wheel gates. If you overshoot, the gate passes the fence. Backing up does not re-engage it. You must start the entire sequence over from the beginning.

"The dial won't turn when I try to open it"

You may have a relock trigger. If the safe was ever attacked or dropped, a secondary relocking mechanism may have engaged. This is a service call — don't try to force it.

"I turned the dial but it feels loose"

If the dial spins freely without engaging the wheels, the drive cam may be broken or disengaged. This also requires a service call.

⚠️ If the dial reaches a hard stop and won't turn further, don't force it. Something is mechanically blocking the lock. Forcing it can break the dial, the spindle, or the lock itself — turning a simple repair into an expensive one.

Self-Help Before You Call

Before you call for a service visit, try these quick checks:

  • Read the serial number on the lock (usually stamped on the lock face or the edge of the dial ring). A locksmith can sometimes look up the original factory combination.
  • Try the factory combination — many locks ship with 50-25-50 or 0-0-0. Worth a shot if you've never changed it.
  • Check for a key-locking dial — some dials have a small keyhole that locks the dial in place. If you don't have the key, the dial won't turn.
  • Try the opposite direction pattern — if L-R-L fails, try R-L-R. Some safes are configured opposite to the norm.
  • Look for a second opening index — rare, but some European safes have the index at 2 o'clock or 4 o'clock.

The Bottom Line

Dialing a mechanical safe lock is a skill — but a simple one once you understand the pattern. The 4-3-2-1 (or 5-4-3-2 for Group 1) counting method works on nearly every mechanical dial lock made in the last 80 years.

If it still doesn't open after following these instructions, the lock may need service — or you may just need a second set of seasoned hands on the dial. That's where I come in.
