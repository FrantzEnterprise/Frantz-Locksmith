That "Cheaper" Safe Guy Just Cost You $7,000

By Robert Frantz • May 2026 • CA LCO 4160

I get called in after the fact a lot. Someone's safe won't open, they call the first locksmith they find on Google, and a few hours later they've got a safe with four drill holes in the side, a triggered relocker they can't reset, and a busted handle they didn't need to touch.

Now they're on the phone with me — and the bill is about to get real.

I've been doing this for 40+ years. GSA certified. Preferred tech for Liberty, Fort Knox, Stealth, Superior, and National. I don't say that to impress you. I say it because I've seen what happens when someone who doesn't know what they're doing gets let loose on a safe. And it's not pretty.

The math is simple: A qualified safe opening starts around $275–$425 depending on the safe type. A repair after an amateur gets involved? That's $500–$2,000 to undo the damage — assuming it can be undone.

❌ This is what an amateur does when they don't know where the lock is. Safe is a total loss.

What Actually Goes Wrong

The Drill Approach — Most amateurs reach for a drill first because they don't know how to manipulate a dial. They punch holes wherever they guess the lock is. Sometimes they hit it. Sometimes they miss and hit the relocker, which fires a second set of bolts that now lock the safe permanently. At that point, you're not getting in without cutting the door open.

The Relocker — Every quality safe has a secondary locking mechanism that activates if someone tries to punch or drill the lock. It's there to stop burglars. But an amateur who doesn't know where the relocker sits will fire it within seconds. Now the safe that was locked with one lock is locked with two — and the second one is harder to reach.

Destroyed Fireproofing — Drill holes punch right through the fire-resistant insulation inside the door. Even if they get the safe open, that fire rating you paid extra for? Gone. If a fire ever hits, your documents, cash, and valuables will burn.

Broken Handles, Bent Bolts, and Pry Bar Damage — I've pulled up to jobs where someone took a crowbar to the door and bent every bolt. Or hammered the handle until the spindle snapped. Now we're not just opening the safe — we're replacing the entire lock body, handle assembly, and sometimes re-sleeving the bolts. That's real money.

A $500 Opening Becomes a $7,000 Replacement

Here's a real scenario I've seen more times than I can count:

  • Cheap guy shows up, drills three holes, misses the lock, fires the relocker. Says "I can't open this." Charges $200 and leaves.
  • I get the call. Now I've got a safe with a triggered relocker, drilled holes through the fire lining, and a lock that's been damaged. I can still open it — but it's going to take longer and cost more than if we'd done it right the first time.
  • The repair bill: Opening fee + relocker replacement + drill hole repair + new lock + handle assembly (if they broke that too). We're talking $800–$1,500 easily.
  • Or worse: The damage is too extensive. The door is compromised. The fire lining is destroyed. Now you're looking at a $3,000–$10,000+ replacement safe, plus delivery, removal of the old one, and anchoring. And you still paid the amateur $200.
I've seen it go past $10,000. A commercial TL-30 safe that got butchered by someone who didn't know the relocker location. That safe was $8,000 new. After the damage, it was a write-off. Plus removal and replacement costs. The customer paid nearly $12,000 for what would have been a $450 professional opening.

What a Real Technician Does Differently

We Listen First — Before I touch a safe, I want to know everything about it. Brand, age, lock type, serial number, how it was acting before it failed. Half the time I can tell you what's wrong over the phone.

We Try Manipulation First — The dial is the first tool I reach for, not the drill. Working the wheel pack takes time and skill, but it leaves the safe undamaged. Most safes can be opened this way.

We Know Where the Relocker Is — Every safe model is different. I know the X-07, X-08, X-09, X-10 GSA containers like I know my own truck. I know where Liberty safes put their relockers. I know Fort Knox, Stealth, Superior, National. I don't have to guess.

When We Do Drill, It's Surgical — One small hole in exactly the right spot. The lock is reached, the safe opens, and the hole gets patched cleanly. Your fire rating is preserved. The safe looks factory when I'm done.

The Other Option: This Is What an Amateur Does

Someone told this safe owner that cutting the side of the safe open was "the only way it can be done." No. It's the only way that guy can do it — because he doesn't know where the lock is, doesn't understand the relocker, and doesn't have the skill to do it right.

❌ The side of the safe was cut open. Safe is destroyed.

That safe is destroyed. The fire lining is gone. The structural integrity is compromised. It will never be as secure as it was. The owner now has to buy a new safe — thousands of dollars — on top of what they paid the amateur.

This is the difference between a safe technician and a guy with a sawzall.
